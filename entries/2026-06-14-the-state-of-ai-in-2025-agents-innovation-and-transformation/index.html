📌 Executive Summary
  • WHAT: McKinsey's 2025 Global Survey on the state of AI (1,993 respondents across 105 nations, fielded June 25–July 29, 2025) reveals that 88% of organizations now regularly use AI in at least one function, up from 78% a year ago.
  • CORE FINDING: Despite widespread AI adoption, most organizations remain in experimentation or piloting phases — only ~one-third have begun scaling AI enterprise-wide, and just 39% report any EBIT-level financial impact.
  • AI AGENTS EMERGING: 62% of respondents say their organizations are at least experimenting with AI agents, and 23% are already scaling agentic AI in at least one function — most heavily in IT and knowledge management.
  • HIGH PERFORMERS STAND OUT: The top ~6% of organizations (AI high performers) are 3x more likely to pursue transformative change, fundamentally redesign workflows, have senior leader ownership, and commit 20%+ of digital budgets to AI.
  • WORKFORCE UNCERTAINTY: 32% of respondents expect enterprise-wide workforce reductions of 3%+ in the next year due to AI, while 43% expect no change and 13% expect increases — signaling significant but unresolved employment disruption ahead.
🏆 Top 5 Takeaways
  1. AI use is widespread but shallow: 88% of organizations use AI in at least one function, yet nearly two-thirds have NOT begun scaling it across the enterprise — the pilot-to-scale transition is stalled for most.
  2. AI agents are gaining traction fast: 62% of respondents' organizations are at least experimenting with AI agents, with agent use most developed in IT (service desk management) and knowledge management (deep research).
  3. Enterprise financial impact is still rare: Only 39% report any EBIT impact attributable to AI, and most of those say less than 5% of EBIT is AI-driven — use-case-level cost and revenue gains exist but don't yet roll up to the bottom line.
  4. Workflow redesign is the single most distinguishing success factor: AI high performers are nearly 3x as likely to have fundamentally redesigned workflows, and this practice 'had one of the strongest contributions to achieving meaningful business impact of all the factors tested.'
  5. Senior leadership ownership is a critical differentiator: High performers are three times more likely to strongly agree that senior leaders demonstrate ownership of and commitment to AI initiatives, including actively role-modeling AI use.
⚡ Actionables
  1. Move beyond efficiency-only objectives: Set growth and innovation as explicit AI goals alongside cost reduction — organizations doing so report higher rates of customer satisfaction improvement, competitive differentiation, revenue growth, and market share change.
  2. Prioritize workflow redesign over tool deployment: Fundamentally redesign individual workflows rather than simply layering AI on existing processes — this is identified as one of the strongest contributors to enterprise-level AI value.
  3. Secure and visibly activate senior leadership commitment: Ensure senior leaders not only endorse AI initiatives but actively role-model AI use and drive adoption — high performers are 3x more likely to have this in place.
  4. Invest proportionally: Commit more than 20% of digital budgets to AI technologies if targeting high-performer status — over one-third of high performers operate at this investment level, enabling faster scaling.
  5. Establish human validation processes: Define clear processes for when and how model outputs require human review for accuracy — this practice is among the top factors distinguishing high performers from the rest.
  6. Expand AI agent pilots into scaling in IT and knowledge management first: These are the functions with the most developed agentic use cases (service desk management, deep research), offering the clearest near-term path to agentic value.

🧠 Thinking Framework
AI Adoption Maturity Curve + Rewired Six-Dimension Management Framework

McKinsey implicitly applies a maturity-curve framework for AI adoption, structured across three stages: (1) Experimentation/Piloting — organizations trying AI in isolated use cases without enterprise integration; (2) Scaling — expanding AI into multiple functions with defined delivery processes; (3) Transformation — fundamentally redesigning workflows and treating AI as a business model catalyst. High performers are distinguished by six management dimensions from McKinsey's 'Rewired' research: Strategy, Talent, Operating Model, Technology, Data, and Adoption & Scaling. The framework positions workflow redesign and leadership commitment as the highest-leverage levers for moving up the maturity curve, ahead of technology investment alone.

🔭 What's Ahead

The survey's conclusion points to three forward-looking signals: (1) As AI tools — including agents — improve and organizational AI capabilities mature, the opportunity to 'embed AI more fully into the enterprise will offer organizations new ways to capture value and create competitive advantage.' (2) Workforce impact is expected to accelerate: the share of respondents anticipating AI-related workforce reductions is projected to nearly double from a median of 17% (past year actuals) to 30% (next year expectations) across business functions. (3) The divergence between AI high performers and the rest is likely to widen — high performers are scaling faster, investing more, and redesigning more aggressively, suggesting a compounding advantage dynamic. The survey implies that the window for organizations to move from piloting to scaling is narrowing, and those that delay workflow transformation risk falling behind peers who are already embedding AI into core business processes at scale.
